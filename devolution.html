Section 01 · Devolution

Four nations, four planning systems

Devolution did not just move power to Edinburgh, Cardiff and Belfast — it built four different machines for deciding whether energy gets built. The clearest fingerprint is who decides: a local council, or the national government.

The decision route

Local hall or national desk?

Most projects are decided locally. But a slice goes through the "national" route — Scotland's Section 36 consents, Westminster's Nationally Significant Infrastructure regime, the Planning Inspectorate. The share that travels that road is wildly different across the four nations.

Devolution in one number

Scotland centralises a quarter of its projects; England barely one in sixty. Holyrood's Section 36 regime pulls large onshore wind and hydro up to national level, while England's councils still decide almost everything below the 50 MW threshold. Northern Ireland routes nothing nationally in this dataset.

By technology

What goes national

The national route is not about nations alone — it is about scale. Offshore wind is entirely national everywhere; rooftop-to-field solar is almost entirely local. Onshore wind sits on the fault line, which is exactly why devolved choices about it matter so much.

The defining chart

The English onshore wind cliff

In 2015 the UK government made it nearly impossible to consent onshore wind in England — a single local objection could block a turbine, and subsidies were pulled. Devolution meant Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland never followed. Watch where the applications go.

Why it matters

This is devolution made visible. The same technology, the same decade — but four political settlements produced four completely different trajectories. England's near-total stall after 2015 was a policy choice, not a planning inevitability, and the devolved nations show the road not taken.
