Section 02 · Party

Does the town hall's party matter?

Each local application is decided by a council, and councils have a political colour. By joining every project to the party that controlled its planning authority in the year the decision was made, we can ask a sharp question: does who runs the council change what gets built? The honest answer — only for the technology people can see from their window.

The contested technology

Onshore wind is where party bites

Strip out the easy approvals and look only at onshore wind — the visible, contested, neighbour-bothering technology. Now the parties separate cleanly, in exactly the order you might expect.

Local control matters here

Conservative-controlled councils approve onshore wind at 61%, against Labour's 69%, the Lib Dems' 71% and the SNP's 75%. A 14-point spread on the one technology where a turbine on the ridgeline is a live local fight.

Credit where it's due

Who actually approved the capacity?

It is tempting to score parties by the renewable capacity sitting in the councils they run today. But control changes hands — a council Reform won in May 2025 may carry a pipeline approved years earlier under someone else, and even within 2025 most permits were granted before the election. The honest question is who held the council on the date each decision was made. Read that way, the picture changes sharply.

The attribution trap

Reform-run councils have actually approved about 0.3 GW. How you count changes the answer by 40×: a present-day snapshot credits Reform with 12.3 GW (pipelines in councils it won in 2025), bucketing by election year gives 3.2 GW, but reading control at the permit date — Reform only took these councils in May 2025, so it cannot own permits granted before then — leaves ~0.3 GW. Conservative councils, governing the most land for the longest, approved the most overall (~51 GW). Decision speed tracks what is being built more than who decides: Labour's solar-and-battery-heavy urban pipeline clears in under three months, while independent-led rural councils, hosting slower and contested onshore wind, take roughly three times as long.

Capacity approved per km² of land controlled — credited at the permit date

2015–2025, lower-tier councils. Each approval is credited to the party controlling the council on the permit date, then normalised by the land that party actually controlled in those years (MW per km², per year).

The land-rich shires build the least

Read this way, the densest approvers are the urban Green and Labour councils — small areas hosting a lot of rooftop solar and batteries — while the land-rich Conservative, SNP and independent-led shires that hold most of Britain's countryside approve the least per acre. Reform now sits mid-pack rather than top: stripped of the inherited pipeline a present-day snapshot wrongly credited it with, its brief tenure is too short and its councils too recently won to register much per km². This is the time-consistent version — no party is credited with consents granted before it controlled the council.

Aligned with Westminster?

Does it matter if the council matches the government?

A natural hypothesis: councils run by the same party as the UK government wave projects through. The data says — barely, overall. And where it does show up, the cause is tangled.

Onshore wind only

Read this descriptively, not causally

Across all technologies, alignment makes almost no difference (90.0% vs 88.8%). For onshore wind, "aligned" councils approve less (60.9% vs 67.1%) — but that is mostly an artefact: the aligned years are dominated by Conservative councils under a Conservative government during the 2015–2023 onshore-wind moratorium. This cut describes a correlation, not a cause.

A note on Northern Ireland

Northern Ireland's councils sit outside the GB charts on this page — NI uses STV power-sharing and a separate, shorter control series, so it isn't part of the Con/Lab story. The four-nations comparisons on the devolution page include NI in full.
