Section 04 · Technology

What Britain is trying to build

What developers propose has shifted repeatedly over fifteen years: the solar surge, the arrival of grid-scale batteries, and the sharp decline of onshore wind in England after 2015.

The shifting pipeline

What the system is asked to process, year by year

Two very different stories, depending on how you count. By applications, solar and batteries swamp everything — thousands of individually small schemes the planning system has to churn through. By capacity, a handful of giant offshore wind farms rival the entire solar fleet. Toggle between them: the gap is the workload problem in one picture.

The two approval stories

Overall approval rates have drifted up to ~90%. That headline sits above onshore wind, whose approval rate fell through the early 2010s and then recovered later in the decade.

Onshore wind

Onshore wind approval fell from ~64% in 2008 to 40% by 2014, around the run-up to the 2015 English restrictions, and has since climbed back above 80%.
