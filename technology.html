Section 04 · Technology

What Britain is trying to build

The mix of what developers propose has been rewritten twice in fifteen years: first the great solar surge, then the arrival of grid-scale batteries. And running underneath it all, the long shadow of the policies that nearly killed onshore wind in England.

The shifting pipeline

Applications, year by year

Solar applications exploded from 2011, crashed when subsidies were cut mid-decade, then roared back. Batteries went from non-existent to a major share after 2017. Onshore wind, once the dominant technology, faded to a trickle.

The divergence

The two approval stories

Overall approval rates have drifted upward to ~90%. But that headline hides onshore wind, whose English approval rate collapsed through the early 2010s as policy turned against it — and only recovered once applications had already migrated to Scotland.

The scar

Onshore wind approval fell from ~64% in 2008 to 40% by 2014 — the years of mounting local-objection powers and the run-up to the 2015 English moratorium. It has since climbed back above 80%, but a decade of building was lost.

The end of the line

Decommissioned: the first generation retires

A small but growing list of early renewable sites — Britain's first commercial wind farm at Delabole, pioneer tidal devices, landfill-gas plants — have now been decommissioned, the leading edge of an asset base that will need replacing.
