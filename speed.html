Section 03 · The Machine

How long does Britain take to say yes?

Approval is the easy part. The harder questions are how fast, for whom, and at what size the system starts to grind. The averages flatter it — because the typical project is a small, quick solar scheme, while the megawatts pile up in slow, large ones.

The averages lie

Counting projects vs counting megawatts

A median solar project is consented in 2.3 months. But weight each project by the capacity it carries, and that figure jumps to nearly 10 months — because the big schemes that hold most of the gigawatts move far more slowly. Across all technologies, the gap is 3.8 months by count versus 14.4 by capacity.

The distortion

Most projects are small and fast; most capacity sits in large, slow schemes. Headline "speed" stats describe the experience of a rooftop installer, not of the gigawatt-scale projects the grid actually needs.

Yes vs no

Refusals take longer than approvals

For most technologies a refusal is slower to arrive than a grant — the contested cases that end in "no" are the ones that drag through committees, objections and deliberation.

The 50 MW cliff

Designed to dodge the national route

In England, a solar scheme above 50 MW is bumped out of the local planning system into the slow, national NSIP / DCO regime. So developers don't build 60 MW projects — they build 49.9 MW ones. The data shows both the time penalty of size, and the unmistakable bunching just below the threshold.

Threshold gaming

209 solar projects sit at 45–49.99 MW; just 5 sit at 50–55 MW. Developers deliberately size schemes at 49.9 MW to stay in the faster local route and out of the Nationally Significant Infrastructure Project regime. The planning threshold is shaping the power stations themselves.

The backlog

What's stuck in the queue

Beyond the decided projects sits a live queue of applications still awaiting a verdict. Most are recent and within reason — but a long tail has been waiting years.
