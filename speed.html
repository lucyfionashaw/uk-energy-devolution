Section 03 · The Machine

How long does Britain take to say yes?

Approval is the easy part. The harder question is how long everything takes — to reach a decision, to break ground, to reach the grid — and how that changes by technology, by project size, and by who runs the council.

The whole timeline

Where the years actually go

Every technology on one time axis, application to switch-on, split into small and large where scale matters. Small solar clears in months; large onshore and offshore wind take years — and most of the wait comes after the planning verdict, not before it. Each stage's bar covers only the projects that reached it (later stages are survivor-selected, so read them as the experience of the ones that got that far).

Duration of each stage, by technology and scale

REPD Q1 2026. Each segment is over projects whose two endpoint dates both exist and fall in order (sample size in the tooltip); a stage is omitted where fewer than 15 qualify. REPD records each date independently, so the stages draw on different, non-nested subsets. Planning permission is the consent in REPD — decision and consent are one event, with no separate consent stage. Stacking stage medians approximates a typical timeline, not one project's actual path.
Size drives the timeline

Where the break by project size is

Split one technology by capacity band and the timeline stretches with size. For solar the jump is sharp around the 50 MW mark — the threshold that bumps a scheme out of the local planning system into the slow national route. Pick a technology to see where its break sits.

Duration by project size band

REPD Q1 2026, capacity bands (Installed Capacity, MWelec). Each segment is over projects in that band with both endpoint dates; small bands and later stages can be thin — see the tooltip.
Does the party matter?

Is it quicker under one party than another?

Credit each decision to the party controlling the council when it was made, and compare how long each stage takes. Labour-run councils decide fastest; but their pipeline skews urban and solar-heavy, so use the technology filter to compare like-for-like. Some parties only have a decision-stage bar — too few, or too newly in control, to have projects that reached construction yet.

The backlog

What's stuck in the queue — and for how long

Beyond the decided projects sits a live queue of applications still awaiting a verdict — some filed months ago, others waiting years, well past the ~4-month statutory target. This is where much of the proposed capacity is held up.
