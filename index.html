UK Energy & Devolution

Who gets to build energy in Britain?

Every wind farm, solar park and battery starts as a planning application. Whether it gets built depends on which nation it sits in, who controls the council, and which of Britain's four planning systems decides its fate. This is the politics of the energy transition, read through 14,000 planning decisions.

Source: Renewable Energy Planning Database (REPD), Q1 2026 · joined to UK council political control, 1973–2026.

The big picture

Proposed, consented — and stuck

Britain has proposed roughly 375 GW of renewable capacity. Only about 58 GW is generating today. The bottleneck is not the planning committee's "no" — once a project reaches a decision it is approved nearly nine times in ten. The capacity is trapped in the queue: tens of gigawatts waiting for a decision, and far more holding consent but never built.

The planning funnel

Every project narrows stage by stage — submitted, decided, approved, under construction, operational. The same journey counted two ways: by number of projects, and by the capacity behind them.

The headline

The system says "yes" — then nothing happens. Of projects that reach a decision, 89% are approved. But ~89 GW is still waiting for a decision, and roughly 154 GW holds a live consent yet has never started construction. The attrition that matters is not refusal; it is everything between "yes" and the grid.

About this dashboard

A non-commercial data project on the politics of Britain's energy transition. The underlying analysis joins the Renewable Energy Planning Database to the political composition of every deciding local authority, and separates locally-decided projects from those consented through the devolved/national routes (Scotland's Section 36, Westminster's NSIP/DCO regime, the Planning Inspectorate and Secretary of State).

Figures cover 14,316 projects representing 375 GW of proposed capacity. Approval rates are calculated on decided applications only.
