UK Energy Planning

Who gets to build energy in Britain?

Every wind farm, solar park and battery starts as a planning application. Whether it ever gets built depends on who controls the council, how long the queue is, and which technology it is. This is the politics of the energy transition, read through 14,000 planning decisions.

Source: Renewable Energy Planning Database (REPD), Q1 2026 · joined to UK council political control, 1973–2026.

The big picture

Proposed, decided — and stuck

Britain has proposed roughly 375 GW of renewable capacity. Only about 58 GW is generating today. The bottleneck is not the planning committee's "no": most of the capacity is trapped before a decision is even made, or holds consent and never gets built. The same journey, counted two ways — by number of projects, and by the capacity behind them.

The bottleneck

What's stuck in the queue — and for how long

Before a project is ever approved or refused it has to be decided, and a growing pile never is. A live queue of applications sits awaiting a verdict — some filed months ago, but a long tail has been waiting years, well past the ~4-month statutory target. This is where the gigawatts actually pile up.

About this dashboard

A non-commercial data project on the politics of Britain's energy transition. The underlying analysis joins the Renewable Energy Planning Database to the political composition of every deciding local authority, and separates locally-decided projects from those consented through the devolved/national routes (Scotland's Section 36, Westminster's NSIP/DCO regime, the Planning Inspectorate and Secretary of State).

Figures cover 14,316 projects representing 375 GW of proposed capacity. Approval rates are calculated on decided applications only.
